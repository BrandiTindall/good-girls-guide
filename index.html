Preface
When Did Being a Good Girl
Become the Enemy?

“When did being a good girl become a bad thing?”

I was having a virtual coffee with my friend Karissa, a woman who has built a remarkable career and is, by every definition in this book, a good girl herself. I was sharing the ethos of this book with her. What it’s about, why I’m writing it, what I believe is true about the women I’ve spent my career alongside (she is one of them).

And she stopped me and asked that question.

I said, “That’s exactly it. That’s the whole reason this book exists.”

Before you begin reading, I want you to sit with that question too.

Because even as the woman who wrote this book, that phrase still lands in my chest like an insult. My ten-year-old son called me a goody two-shoes not long ago. I didn’t let it slide. I asked him why. He shrugged and said, “Just because you are, Mom.”

He didn’t mean it as a compliment. And that’s exactly the problem. Somewhere along the way, being good, being conscientious, being the woman who shows up and holds it all together, became something to be ashamed of and teased for.

He learned that from the world we built.

This book aims to disrupt it. Because when enough women, and enough men, understand what being a good girl actually means, maybe the next generation of ten-year-olds will say it differently.

Maybe they’ll say it and understand that good girls don’t just survive this world. They’re the ones quietly building it. Always were. And that, my friends, is greatness.

Introduction
The Good Girl’s Guide to Greatness

You’ve done everything right.

You worked hard and showed up and were kind, dependable, always prepared. You built the relationships, held the team together, kept the peace. You pushed through the exhaustion with a smile on your face because that’s what you do, because that’s what you’ve always done, because somewhere along the way you decided that being the one who holds it all together is just who you are.

And somehow, despite all of it, you still feel like you’re playing catch-up. Still second-guessing yourself in rooms where you’re clearly the most capable person. Still saying yes when every cell in your body is screaming no. Still burning through everything you have and wondering, quietly, in the moments when nobody else is watching, why it never feels like enough.

This book is for you.

Not the version of you who has it all figured out. The version of you who is lying awake at 2am running the numbers and the conversation and everything you could have done differently. The version of you who is absolutely, undeniably killing it, and somehow still doesn’t fully believe it.

* * *

It started long before the business.

It was the fifth grade and I was colouring a map of Canada, rainbow colours, each province outlined perfectly and shaded in with care. Red for BC, orange for Alberta. I was in a full state of flow, completely absorbed in my own little piece of art, the kind of focus that feels like the rest of the world has gone quiet.

My friend Abella leaned over and asked what I thought of hers. I glanced at it, said it was nice, and went back to mine.

That was it. That was all I did.

What happened next blindsided me completely. Her face hardened. She snapped. She started telling the other girls I had insulted her work, and no matter what I said, no matter how many times I tried to explain, she wouldn’t hear me. She turned my best friend against me, then the rest of the girls, and within a day I was the villain of the fifth grade.

I was crushed.

And I spiraled, the way only a girl who has already learned that other people’s emotions are her responsibility can spiral. What did I say? What did I miss? I should have complimented her more. I should have looked longer. I should have been more careful. It didn’t matter that I hadn’t done anything wrong. The damage to my nervous system was already done, and the lesson my eleven-year-old brain extracted from that moment would quietly run in the background of every relationship I had for the next thirty years.

And from that day forward, I made a silent vow: I will never let something like this happen again.

I bet you know exactly what I mean. I bet you have your own version of this story. Maybe it was a classroom. Maybe it was a family dinner. Maybe it was your first job, or your first business partner, or the woman on your team who smiled to your face and then went around you. The details are different.

The wiring is the same.

Now think about that same moment, but with boys. A boy asks his friend what he thinks of his map, his friend says it’s good, and they move on. No spiraling. No fallout. No six-week campaign to restore his reputation with the rest of the class.

He just moves on.

We don’t get to do that. We never did.

* * *

Here’s what nobody told us about growing up good.

There’s a story from Greek mythology about a woman named Cassandra. She was gifted with the power of prophecy, she could see the truth and the patterns and the future that no one else could see, and she was brilliant and she was right, consistently, about everything.

But she was cursed. No one would believe her.

She saw clearly. She spoke the truth. And her voice was dismissed and diminished and brushed off as madness or drama or being too much.

That is the good girl experience in a single myth.

You read the room better than anyone in it. You sense when something is off before anyone else does. You anticipate what people need before they know they need it. You carry the emotional weight of every relationship you’re in, at home, at work, everywhere, and you do it so seamlessly that most people don’t even notice it’s happening.

And yet the world has consistently rewarded you for being nice, not wise. For being available, not visionary. For managing other people’s comfort instead of trusting your own knowing. We’ve been conditioned to believe that our goodness is tied to silence, that our empathy is a liability, that our sensitivity is weakness, that our knowing is just being dramatic.

But what we’ve been told is “too much” is actually our power.

Our sensitivity is intuition. Our people-pleasing began as protection. Our perfectionism was a survival strategy in a world that moved the goalposts every time we got close. None of it came from nowhere. All of it made sense. And none of it makes you broken.

Being a good girl is not the problem.

Being only the good girl, without boundaries, without voice, without self, is a fast road to burnout.

* * *

I know what that road costs. I walked it all the way to the end.

I built a multi-million dollar business from my basement with two kids under three, no roadmap, no safety net, just an idea and what I can only describe as a relentless, slightly terrifying amount of drive. My business partner and I grew it into something that reached hundreds of thousands of women across North America, and together we hit milestones that fewer than 2% of women in business ever reach.

I’m telling you that number because it matters to what comes next, not because I need you to be impressed.

Because here’s the truth underneath all of it, the thing I wouldn’t have admitted to you back then, the quiet engine running behind every decision and every milestone and every year of sixteen-hour days:

I will prove to everybody that I can make it.

That was the fuel. Not passion, not purpose, not some clear-eyed vision of the woman I wanted to become. The fuel was proof. Proof for the people who doubted me. Proof for the culture that told women like me there was a ceiling. Proof, if I’m really honest, for the eleven-year-old girl who spiraled over a map of Canada and vowed she would never be caught out again.

And I did it. By every external measure, I made it.

And then I watched it crash.

Not slowly. All at once. And in the wreckage, sitting in the rubble of everything my business partner and I had built and everything I had sacrificed and everything I had told myself it was all for, I had to ask myself the question I had been successfully avoiding for nineteen years:

Who am I if I’m not achieving something?

That question undid me. And then, eventually, it rebuilt me. Because what I found in the answer is the reason this book exists.

The crash gave me a lens I didn’t have before. One that finally let me see that every single thing I had called a flaw, the hypervigilance, the people-pleasing, the perfectionism, the relentless need to prove, was never weakness. It was power. Extraordinary, real, earned power. Pointed outward at everyone else’s approval, burning through everything I had, until there was nothing left for me.

That shift changed everything. This book is the direct result of it.

I’ve spent thirteen years as an entrepreneur and six years before that in corporate banking, and I know what it takes to succeed in this world. But more importantly, I know what it costs. Not the cost that shows up in the profit and loss statement. The other one. The one you pay in your health and your relationships and your sense of self and the slow forgetting of who you actually are underneath all the achieving. And I’m here so you don’t have to pay the same price I did.

I’m not sharing this from a pedestal. I’m sharing it from the trenches, from the sleepless nights and the tears behind closed doors and the rebuilding of self-worth again and again, and from learning, finally, that the version of me I kept trying to erase was the version I needed most.

* * *

Here’s the problem with every other “good girl” personal development and leadership book out there.

They tell you to stop. Stop people pleasing. Stop being so agreeable. Stop caring what everyone thinks. Just stop, as if you could flip a switch and undo a lifetime of wiring. As if the traits that kept you safe and made you excellent and carried you this far are simply bad habits you can decide to drop.

You can’t. And you shouldn’t have to. Because here’s what those books don’t understand: your good girl traits aren’t flaws. They’re strategies. Strategies that were formed in response to real experiences and real threats and real consequences, strategies that have carried you further than most women ever get, and strategies that, with the right understanding, can take you further still.

The problem isn’t the programming. The problem is that no one has ever shown you how to update it.

Think about a computer. If it’s been coded to produce one result, you don’t sit down at the keyboard and yell at it to change. You add new code. You build on top of what’s already there and give the system better inputs so it can produce better outputs.

That’s this book. Not erasure. Evolution. New code, built on the foundation of who you already are.

* * *

Have you ever watched the show Dexter?

Dexter is a man born into darkness, shaped by trauma, carrying urges he didn't choose and couldn't suppress. His adoptive father didn't try to erase him or shame him out of who he was. Instead, he gave him a code. A framework for channeling everything he already was into something with direction and purpose.

Dark origin story and all, the principle is actually kind of brilliant. Instead of trying to erase the parts of yourself you were told were too much, what if someone helped you build a code around them instead? That's the idea. That's it. Nobody's getting hurt. Except maybe your competition.

And then there’s Mr. Miyagi.

Okay, if you were born in the 80’s, and I was, you didn’t just watch The Karate Kid. You lived it. Repeated viewings. Quoting it at recess. Genuinely believing that if someone handed you a car to wax, something profound was about to happen to you. Mr. Miyagi didn’t push Daniel-san to be louder or harder or more aggressive. He didn’t hand him a trophy or tell him to toughen up. He handed him a sponge and said wax on, wax off, and what looked like chores were actually lessons in presence and rhythm and power. He taught Daniel to trust what he already had inside him, and to stop trying to fight like someone he wasn’t.

I think about that movie more than I’d like to admit as a grown woman running a business. Because that’s exactly the kind of guide I needed. Not someone telling me to be different. Someone showing me how to finally use what I already was.

That’s what I’m here to do with you.

I’m not here to make you into someone else. I’m here to help you channel who you already are, sharper and clearer and finally pointed in the right direction.

* * *

Because here’s what’s actually true.

You were never the problem. You were always the solution, just aimed at everyone else’s life.

The intuition you’ve been told to doubt? That’s your edge. The empathy you’ve been told makes you weak? That’s your superpower. The warmth and the loyalty and the fierce, relentless care that you pour into everything and everyone around you? That’s not the thing that held you back.

That’s the thing that’s going to take you further than you’ve ever been.

But only if someone finally shows you how to aim it.

* * *

So I went looking for the book that would show me how.

The book that named what we were actually up against. Not a softer version of what already existed, not the same power manual with a feminine cover, but something built from scratch, from the inside out, from the truth of how women actually move through the world. I started where most people start. Amazon. The business section. Reading titles, reading chapter headings, looking for the manual I hadn’t found yet.

And I landed on The 48 Laws of Power.

Maybe you’ve heard of it. Millions of copies sold, referenced by CEOs and athletes and politicians, considered canonical and essential, handed to women in business by mentors and assigned in MBA programs and stacked on the shelves of every airport bookstore in the world. A manual, they say, for how power actually works.

I read the chapter titles.

Never Outshine the Master.

Crush Your Enemy Totally.

Always Say Less Than Necessary.

Raise Your Value Through Absence and Scarcity.

Keep People Dependent on You.

And I felt angry.

Not inspired. Not challenged. Angry. Because these are the bestsellers. These are the books we get handed. And the rules don’t work the same way for us, they just don’t, it’s not the same game. The laws aren’t wrong exactly. They just have a footnote that nobody mentions.

Results may vary depending on the body you’re in.

The game was never designed for us. Not because women aren’t capable of power, obviously we are, but because the entire lineage of power literature was written by men, about men, in a world where men don’t pay the particular tax that women pay just for existing in it. And until someone names that tax out loud, we keep assuming the problem is us.

Let me show you exactly what I mean.

* * *

I was eighteen years old, working a Friday night shift in the lounge.

Skimpy outfit, short skirt, low-cut top. That was the dress code, not my choice, just the uniform I put on and worked in. Ten tables, just me, a full section on a packed Friday night, and I was a busy bee flitting from table to table, reading every person in the room before they had to ask me for anything. That was my gift. I just knew how to be with people, how to make them feel seen and comfortable and taken care of, and I was very good at it.

I remember the couple clearly. He ordered for her and answered for her, and every question I directed at her he intercepted before she could open her mouth. So I kept looking at her, specifically and deliberately at her. What would you like? How do you like your steak cooked? Trying to give her the small dignity of being asked what she actually wanted. I thought nothing of it after they left. I had nine other tables and a full section to run.

The next day my boss asked me to sit down with him.

I was eighteen years old and I sat down in that chair and my very first thought, before he had said a single word, was what did I do wrong? That’s the thing about being a Good Girl at eighteen. Your default position is always that you are the problem, before anyone has said anything, before you have any information at all. You have already started building the case against yourself.

He told me a complaint had come in. The wife. She said I had been flirting with her husband.

I remember the shame that moved through me first, that hot, fast, full-body shame that arrives before your brain has had a chance to think. Oh no. Was I? And then, right behind it, came clarity. Because I remembered that table exactly. I remembered trying to include a woman who was being erased by the man sitting across from her. I remembered the deliberate eye contact and the redirected questions. I had been trying to see her, and somehow that had become a complaint about me.

My boss asked what I remembered. I told him everything.

He was quiet for a moment, and then he said: Yeah. I can see that. He told me I was an excellent communicator, that my warmth and my natural attentiveness to people was the reason I was exceptional at my job, and that sometimes those qualities get misconstrued by someone who is insecure. He was going to chalk it up to that.

Four words. I can see that. I felt them land somewhere in my chest like something I hadn’t known I was waiting for. Someone in authority had looked at the situation clearly and seen what was actually happening, and it wasn’t me.

I moved on. Mostly.

But I carried it, the way you carry things that happen to you at eighteen when you don’t yet have the language for what they were. It didn’t feel like a wound exactly. It felt more like a new awareness, a slight recalibration, a voice that started running quietly in the background asking: how am I coming across? Is this too much? Am I reading this right? You don’t notice it happening. You just gradually become a woman who monitors herself, and you call it professionalism.

* * *

Years later I was at a conference in Calgary, married now, a professional, a woman who had long since proven she knew how to conduct herself.

I was talking to a colleague over drinks, being exactly who I have always been. Warm, friendly, present, naturally at ease with people. My boss appeared at my elbow and told me to be careful. I had a husband at home. He said it in that tone, the one that wraps control inside concern so cleanly that you almost don’t catch it.

And for just one second I felt it again, that eighteen-year-old shame rising up before I could stop it. Oh no, was I…

No.

This time the rage arrived before the shame could finish its sentence. Who are you to manage me. I am in control of myself. I held my ground and told him exactly how I felt, and when we were back at the bank on Monday morning, I told him again.

But here’s what I need you to sit with.

I shouldn’t have had to.

* * *

Same woman. Same warmth. Same natural way of being with people that has always been my greatest professional strength. Two different men, years apart, two completely different contexts.

Same bill.

That is the double tax.

A man having drinks with a colleague at a conference is networking, building relationships, being good at his job. I do the exact same thing and suddenly someone needs to remind me I have a husband at home. The warmth that made me excellent, that my first boss saw clearly and named out loud, becomes evidence against me the moment someone else feels threatened by it. The same move, two completely different prices.

This is what nobody tells you when they hand you the power manual. Every law in that book carries a surcharge for women, not because we play it wrong, but because we play it in a world that charges us extra just for showing up. And most of us, without a single conversation to make sense of it, without anyone ever naming what was happening, quietly started making ourselves smaller. We monitored our warmth. We dimmed the exact thing that made us good. Not because we were told to.

Because we did the math.

We didn’t fail to learn the rules.

We learned very early that the rules would cost us more than they were worth.

* * *

That is why this book exists.

Not to teach you the 48 Laws with a feminine spin, not to tell you to be harder or colder or more strategic or more like the version of power that was never designed with you in mind. To do something that hasn’t been done yet. To take the Good Girl wiring you’ve spent your whole life apologising for, the warmth, the hyperawareness, the people-reading, the deep ethics, the fierce loyalty, and show you that none of it was ever the obstacle.

It was always the weapon.

You just needed someone to show you how to aim it. And that someone didn’t exist yet.

So here we are.

* * *

Let’s begin.
